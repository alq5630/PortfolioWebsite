Empathy Spectrum Disorder (ESD) Framework

Format: Theoretical framework, assessment toolkit, and educational website (in progress)
Role: Creator, primary researcher, writer, and instructional designer

Overview

The Empathy Spectrum Disorder (ESD) framework grew out of my graduate research on empathy, successful psychopathy, trauma, and emotional regulation. It is an attempt to describe the different ways empathy can become dysregulated across a spectrum rather than as a simple lack or excess of empathy. The model considers affective empathy, cognitive empathy, nervous system regulation, and behavior together.

ESD is designed as a conceptual and educational tool rather than a formal diagnosis at this stage. The framework currently includes a white paper under journal review, a multi part assessment toolkit, a clinician facing training curriculum, and an in progress public facing website that translates the ideas into language that practitioners, parents, and learners can actually use.

Core questions this framework addresses

  • Why do some people feel overwhelmed by others while others feel almost nothing at all
  • How do hyperempathy and hypoempathy show up differently in thought, body, and behavior
  • What patterns of nervous system regulation sit underneath common empathy problems
  • How can we talk about psychopathy and narcissism without collapsing all low empathy into one box
  • What tools can help clinicians and educators map empathy patterns without pathologizing everything

Subtype model

The framework organizes empathy dysregulation into a small set of subtypes that describe how affective, cognitive, and regulatory patterns combine. These are not labels for people as much as maps of recurring patterns that show up in real lives and relationships.

  • Type A: Emotional Hypo Empathy – muted emotional resonance, often paired with intact or strong cognitive empathy
  • Type B: Emotional Hyper Empathy – intense emotional resonance, somatic flooding, difficulty separating self from others
  • Type C: Cognitive Dominant Empathy – strong perspective taking with limited emotional resonance, highly rational style
  • Type D: Empathic Dysregulation – oscillation between over engagement and shutdown, often linked to fawn and freeze patterns
  • Type E: Empathic Shutdown – protective blunting of empathy in response to trauma or chronic overwhelm
  • Type F: Dark Empathy – strategic use of cognitive empathy in ways that can be manipulative or harmful

The subtype model is used as a teaching and reflection tool with clinicians, students, and parents. It is designed to deepen nuance rather than create new boxes to keep people in.

Assessment and education toolkit

Alongside the conceptual framework, I am building a multi part toolkit for clinicians and educators. Each tool focuses on a different layer of empathy, regulation, and behavior. Together, they form a structured way to explore how empathy works in a person’s life.

Key tools in development

  • ESDI – Empathy Spectrum Disorder Inventory, a self report instrument that screens for subtype patterns
  • CAES R – Cognitive Affective Empathy Scale revision that distinguishes emotional resonance from perspective taking
  • PPA – Pattern and Profile Assessment that maps subtype traits and coping strategies
  • NEA – Narrative Empathy Analysis that works with stories instead of just numbers
  • ESD SIA – Structured Interview and Analysis guide for practitioners

Educational formats

  • White paper that lays out the theoretical framework and supporting research
  • Slide decks and visual diagrams for teaching in workshops and classes
  • Clinician training modules that pair concepts with real case vignettes
  • Parent and caregiver workbooks written in accessible language
  • Planned digital tools and interactive versions of the inventories

In progress website

The ESD framework is also becoming a public facing website that will eventually include guided explanations, interactive tools, and resources for clinicians, educators, and families. The site is being built from scratch with attention to accessibility, clear navigation, and trauma aware tone.

  • Landing pages for each subtype with plain language descriptions
  • Downloadable handouts and visual summaries of the spectrum
  • Interactive self reflection prompts and journaling questions
  • Sections tailored for clinicians, parents, and curious learners
  • Integration with online courses and CE trainings in future phases

  • Wrote the primary white paper and related manuscripts
  • Designed the structure and content of the assessment toolkit
  • Created visual diagrams and teaching slides for live presentations

Future directions

Next steps for the ESD framework include formal validation studies for the assessment tools, expansion of the clinician training curriculum, and the development of parent and educator tracks. I am also interested in collaborating with researchers and practitioners who want to explore applications of the model in clinical, educational, and community settings.
